## Why these are treated differently

Most of the Tafsir pathway — Safwat al-Tafasir through Tafsir al-Jalalayn, al-Nasafi, and
al-Baydawi — is a *sequence*: each text is meant to be studied roughly after the one before it.

The texts on this page are different. They were named in the source curriculum not as a
sequence, but as a set of works a serious student of tafsir is expected to eventually read
in full, each for a different reason — historical primacy, a particular method, a particular
era, or a particular strength. They are read alongside or after the main pathway, not instead
of it, and not necessarily in the order listed below.

## The essential tafsirs

**Tafsir al-Tabari**, by Ibn Jarir al-Tabari — one of the earliest and most foundational
comprehensive tafsirs, gathering the exegetical opinions of the early generations. Effectively
the trunk that much of the later tradition branches from.

**Tafsir al-Wahidi**, by al-Wahidi — a classical tafsir particularly valued for its treatment
of *asbab al-nuzul*, the occasions of revelation.

**Nadhm al-Durar fi Tanasub al-Ayat wa al-Suwar**, by al-Biqa'i — a distinctive tafsir focused
specifically on *tanasub*: the internal coherence and relationships between verses and chapters
of the Qur'an, a lens most other tafsirs don't foreground.

**Al-Tahrir wa al-Tanwir**, by Muhammad al-Tahir ibn 'Ashur — a major modern comprehensive
tafsir known for combining literary and rhetorical analysis with the classical exegetical
tradition.

**Ruh al-Ma'ani**, by al-Alusi — a comprehensive classical-era tafsir known for synthesizing an
unusually wide range of exegetical, legal, and mystical material in one work.

**Irshad al-'Aql al-Salim**, by Abu al-Su'ud — a classical tafsir known for its rhetorical and
literary sensitivity to the Qur'anic text.

**Al-Tafsir al-Wasit**, by Muhammad al-Tantawi — a modern comprehensive tafsir by the former
Grand Mufti and Shaykh al-Azhar, useful as a contemporary voice alongside the classical works
above.

**Fi Zilal al-Qur'an**, by Sayyid Qutb — a widely read modern tafsir, notable for its literary
and thematic approach to the text rather than a verse-by-verse legal or grammatical treatment.

**Tafsir al-Manar**, by Muhammad Abduh and Rashid Rida — an influential early-modern tafsir
associated with the Islamic reform movement of the late 19th and early 20th centuries, useful
for understanding how that movement read the Qur'an.
