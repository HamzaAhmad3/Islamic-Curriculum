## Review with understanding, not just repetition

Review (*tikrar*) the key concepts — *shurut*, *arkan*, *ta'rifat* — with both understanding and
memorization. An excellent text for this kind of review is al-Yaqut al-Nafis, which essentially
lists definitions, conditions, and pillars in a compact form. It holds a special place in Tarim,
where some teachers set exams that simply ask students to regurgitate these three categories from
memory. They should sit at the heart of any review schedule.

Structure your review sessions around your available time — for example, Sundays for *ibadat*,
Mondays for *jinayat*, Tuesdays for *al-ahwal al-shakhsiyyah*, and so on. As a general rule, aim to
complete a full review of any book you've studied at least once every four months.

## Keep a notebook, and diagram what you learn

Keep a dedicated notebook for each book you're studying, and build your own diagrams to visualize
concepts as you go — this is a form of active engagement that pure reading doesn't give you. Review
these notes alongside your textual review. Al-Taqrirat al-Sadidah is an excellent model to compare
your own diagrams and charts against; some of its layouts can even become inspiration for your own.
'Imad 'Ali Jumu'ah's Tashjir series is a superb example of what this looks like at its best — it
takes famous classical texts and re-renders them entirely in diagram form.

## Read beyond the assigned text

Seek guidance from your teachers about supplementary readings. This matters more than it might
seem — targeted, extensive reading alongside your formal textbook widens and deepens understanding
in a way that the primary text alone often can't. As a working example: while reading Minhaj
al-Talibin, it's worth personally reading Rawdat al-Talibin issue by issue alongside it. The two
complement each other closely.

## Prepare before class, not after

Prepare for each class by reading the forthcoming section of the text ahead of time — this also
helps you arrive with real questions. A useful ratio to aim for is roughly 3:1: for every hour of
formal class, put in about three hours of preparation beforehand.

As a concrete example — if you're studying Matn Abi Shuja' and the next class covers *bab umm
al-walad*, read the relevant section ahead of time across several commentaries: Hashiyat al-Bajuri,
al-Iqna', and Kifayat al-Akhyar. Compare how each elucidates the same issue, and compile your own
summarized commentary drawing from all three.

## Practice applying rulings to real cases

Think in terms of *taswir* — concrete, practical scenarios that force you to apply what you've
learned. For example, in matters of inheritance (*mirath*): what is the estate division if the
deceased left behind a mother, a son, a daughter, and a wife? Work through your own hypothetical
cases, and real ones you're aware of, rather than only ever engaging with the ruling in the
abstract.

## Understand the reasoning, selectively

Where possible, understand the *ta'lil* — the underlying reasoning — behind a ruling, and consider
the questions that naturally arise from it. Nihayat al-Matlab by Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni is one
of the best texts for this kind of depth.

For instance: the reasoning behind washing one's hands upon waking, before placing them in a basin,
traces back to the fact that the Sahaba's standard practice was to perform *istijmar* (rather than
*istinja'*), and they often slept unclothed in the heat — meaning traces of urine or stool could
plausibly have touched their hands during sleep. This raises a natural follow-up question worth
sitting with: if a person performs *istinja'* and sleeps clothed, does the same ruling on washing
the hands upon waking still apply?

Al-Majmu', al-Bayhaqi's works, and al-Mu'tamad are excellent for tracing how a given ruling was
actually arrived at from its evidence. That said, be selective and efficient about what you commit
to memory here — most students should only memorize the evidence for genuinely contentious issues,
not points of near-universal agreement. There's little value in memorizing the evidence for the
obligation of fasting Ramadan, for instance, since virtually every school agrees on it — but the
evidence for why touching a marriageable member of the opposite sex invalidates ritual purity is
exactly the kind of contested issue worth knowing well.

## Write your own companion book

From your notes and readings, eventually write your own companion book covering everything you've
studied. This becomes a genuinely useful personal reference for future questions of *masa'il* — and
if it turns out well, it's worth sharing with others too.
