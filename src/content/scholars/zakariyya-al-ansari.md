Zakariyya al-Ansari rose from poverty in the Egyptian countryside to become Chief Judge (Qadi al-Qudat) of Egypt and one of the most prolific and widely taught scholars of his era, reportedly living past the age of one hundred. He studied hadith intensively under Ibn Hajar al-'Asqalani and tafsir and usul under Jalal al-Din al-Mahalli, before going on to personally abridge Minhaj al-Talibin into his own primer, Manhaj al-Tullab, and separately produce major commentaries in several other textual lineages — Asna al-Matalib on Rawd al-Talib, Tuhfat al-Tullab on his own Tahrir Tanqih al-Lubab, and al-Ghurar al-Bahiyyah on Ibn al-Wardi's al-Bahjah al-Wardiyyah. His most lasting legacy may be pedagogical rather than textual: both Ibn Hajar al-Haytami and Shams al-Din al-Ramli studied under him before going on to become the two authorities whose commentaries later defined the school's relied-upon positions.
