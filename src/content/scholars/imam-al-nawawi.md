Yahya ibn Sharaf al-Nawawi devoted an unusually short life — he died at 44 — to an enormous body of writing that reshaped how the Shafi'i school would be studied for the next seven centuries. He settled in Damascus as a young man, studied intensively under the leading scholars of his generation, and by his thirties was already producing the works that would define his legacy: a concise creedal and legal primer culture (Minhaj al-Talibin) so widely commented upon that "the mu'tamad position" in later Shafi'i fiqh is often simply whichever reading of al-Nawawi's text a given commentator settles on. Alongside his legal writing he produced two of the most widely read hadith compilations in Islamic history, Riyadh al-Salihin and the Forty Hadith, both still commonly among the first substantial texts a student of hadith encounters.
